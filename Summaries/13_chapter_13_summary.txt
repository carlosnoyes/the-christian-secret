Chapter 13 Summary: Failures
=============================

**Main Idea**:
Failures and sins may occur in the life of faith, but they need not be fatal or permanent. The key is to instantly return to God with confession and trust rather than sinking into prolonged discouragement. Sanctification is not a fixed state but a daily walk, and the path can always be regained after a stumble.

**Key Points**:
- Sin ceases to be a necessity in the life of faith, but it remains a possibility; conscious, known sin is what is being addressed, not sins of ignorance.
- When a believer sins, two dangerous responses must be avoided: utter discouragement and giving up, or covering the sin up and calling it mere "infirmity."
- The proper response is to face the sin honestly, confess it immediately to God, believe in His instant forgiveness, and trust more fully than before.
- Discouragement after failure is not humility -- it is the enemy's temptation; God's command is always "Up, sanctify the people."
- The moment of recognizing sin should also be the moment of recognizing forgiveness; no delay or prolonged penance is needed.
- After confession and forgiveness, believers must forget the sin and press forward, not dwell on it or magnify it.
- The root cause of failures is often a hidden, unconsecrated area in the heart -- something contrary to God's will that has not been surrendered.
- Suffering and remorse over failures can themselves be rooted in self-love rather than genuine concern for God's glory.
- The old mystic writers teach that a simple, peaceful turning to God aids progress far more than chagrin and self-reproach.

**Illustrations and Examples**:
- A child learning to walk: just as a child who falls must get up and try again, so must the believer who falls into sin.
- Israel's defeat at Ai (Joshua 7): their defeat was not due to the enemy's strength but to Achan's hidden sin; once the accursed thing was removed, victory followed.
- A little girl's theology of forgiveness: she insisted God would make people feel sorry for days before forgiving them, illustrating the common but mistaken belief that God delays forgiveness.
- A mother's response to a naughty child: God, like a loving mother, yearns for the child who runs back immediately rather than the one who hides in despairing remorse.
- The discouraged Christian man: after one unkind act, he spiraled into three years of despair until a Christian lady showed him 1 John 1:9 and he claimed forgiveness by faith in minutes.
- The Christian lady overcome by anger: she immediately confessed while still boiling with anger, claimed God's forgiveness aloud, and was delivered within five minutes.
- The moth-infested cask in the cellar: an innocent-looking sealed cask, left uninvestigated for years, turned out to be the source of a moth infestation -- illustrating how hidden, unexamined sins cause spiritual defeat.
- A headache as symptom: outward sin is often only a symptom of a deeper, hidden evil in another part of one's being.
