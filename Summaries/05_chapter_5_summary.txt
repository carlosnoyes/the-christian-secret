Chapter 5 Summary: Difficulties Concerning Consecration
========================================================

Main Idea
---------
The chief difficulty in consecration is that believers wait to *feel* consecrated before they believe it has happened, when God's order is always faith first and feeling second. Once a believer makes a definite act of surrender, he must reckon it as an accomplished fact and never take it back, because the very act of giving something to God makes it "most holy unto the Lord." The chapter provides practical steps for settling the matter of consecration once and for all through a decisive act of the will and ongoing faith.

Key Points
----------
- The enemy's primary tactic is to make believers depend on feelings rather than faith. God's invariable rule is faith first, feeling second.
- Consecration is a definite, settled transaction. Once you give yourself to God, you must believe He has received you and never reopen the question.
- Repeatedly re-consecrating yourself out of doubt is like giving an estate to a friend and then coming back day after day to re-give it -- it creates endless confusion and dishonors both parties.
- Under Old Testament law, every devoted thing became "most holy unto the Lord" by the act of giving, regardless of the giver's feelings. The holiness came from the Receiver, not the giver's sincerity.
- It is not your feelings about the surrender that matter, but your will and purpose. God looks at your purpose, not your emotions.
- Once consecration is settled, God takes possession and begins to "work in you to will and to do of His good pleasure."
- Doubt and discouragement are always from the enemy and must be refused instantly.

Illustrations and Examples
--------------------------
- The estate analogy: Giving an estate to a friend is a mental transaction taken on faith. If you kept returning day after day to re-give it, your friend would doubt you ever meant it, and you yourself would be hopelessly confused about who owned it.
- The Old Testament offering: An Israelite who placed an offering on the altar could not take it back. The altar sanctified the gift. All Israel would have been aghast at someone who tried to reclaim a devoted thing.
- The imagined offerer: A man who brought a gift to the priest and then returned saying he was not sure he had given it sincerely would be told the matter was already settled by God's own law.
