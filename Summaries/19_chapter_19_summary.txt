Chapter 19 Summary: Kings and Their Kingdoms; or, How to Reign in the Interior Life
=====================================================================================

Main Idea
---------
The kingdom of God is an interior kingdom set up within the soul, not an outward
display of pomp and power. True kingship in this kingdom is exercised through
meekness, service, childlikeness, and self-sacrifice -- the very opposite of the
world's idea of ruling -- and those who humble themselves and serve others are the
ones who truly reign.

Key Points
----------
- The kingdom of God is within; it is a condition of the soul, not a place.
  Its thrones are inward thrones of dominion over the things of time and sense.
- The inward always rules the outward: inner peace cannot be disturbed by
  outward turmoil, but outward peace can never quiet an inward tempest.
- The government is upon Christ's shoulders, not ours; He bears all the weight,
  care, and responsibility of kingship, freeing His subjects from anxiety.
- God's kingdom conquers by inward power, not outward conflict; He subdues
  enmity itself, not merely enemies, winning hearts in willing surrender.
- The kingdom is characterized by righteousness, peace, and joy in the Holy
  Ghost, and is "not in word, but in power."
- True reigning means being inwardly a king while outwardly a servant; the
  soul that serves is the soul that reigns. The greatest is the servant of all.
- Entry into this kingdom requires becoming as a little child: guileless,
  trustful, obedient, and self-forgetting.
- The path to the throne is through death to self and resurrection into the
  life of Christ; suffering with Him leads to reigning with Him.

Illustrations and Examples
--------------------------
- Diogenes told "The king has degraded you," who replied, "but I am not
  degraded!" -- demonstrating that no outward act can degrade an inwardly
  free soul.
- Ulysses filling his crew's ears with wax and binding himself to the mast to
  pass the sirens, versus Orpheus who made sweeter music that enabled his crew
  to pass with disdain -- illustrating the difference between outward
  restraint and inward triumph.
- The monk of St. Cyr who endured cruel persecution with unfailing kindness
  and meekness until he won over his enemies and became their trusted leader;
  then conquered a violent criminal through love alone.
- Daniel in the lions' den and the three Hebrew children in the fiery furnace:
  God's way was not to prevent the trial but to triumph through it.
- Joseph reigning as a king in slavery and prison before ascending to
  Pharaoh's throne.
- A boy from a Refuge in England who saved a drowning comrade and received a
  medal from the Queen, his self-forgetful courage commanding an audience of
  grown men -- illustrating how Christ-like self-sacrifice creates kingship.
