Chapter 8 Summary: Is God in Everything?
=========================================

Main Idea
---------
One of the greatest obstacles to a life of entire surrender and trust is the difficulty of seeing God in everything, especially in trials that come through human instrumentality. The chapter argues that for the children of God there are no "second causes" -- everything that reaches them has first passed through God's hands and received His permission. Once a believer grasps this truth, it becomes impossible to murmur against circumstances or people, and life becomes one long thanksgiving marked by an unshakable rest of heart.

Key Points
----------
- Many believers say they can submit to things from God but not to things from people. Yet nearly everything in life comes through human instrumentalities -- failures, ignorance, carelessness, and sin.
- For God's children, nothing can touch them without first passing through the Father's hands. What originates as man's sin, by the time it reaches the believer, has become God's will for that person and must be accepted from His hand.
- The whole teaching of Scripture affirms God's sovereign control: not a sparrow falls without the Father; the hairs of our heads are numbered; He turns the hearts of kings; He rules the raging sea.
- Things in which we can see God's hand have a sweetness that consoles even while they wound. But trials seen as coming only from man are full of bitterness. Seeing God in everything removes the bitterness.
- This truth alone puts an end to all murmuring. Christians often feel free to murmur against man while they would not dare murmur against God. Seeing God behind all things eliminates that distinction.
- Trials can become "chariots" that bear us to the very victories we have prayed for, if we mount into them rather than letting them crush us.
- God does not order the wrong thing, but He uses it for our blessing.

Illustrations and Examples
--------------------------
- The earthly father and his child: If a child is in its father's arms, nothing can touch it without the father's consent. If something harmful comes, the father suffers it first in his own person. How much more does our Heavenly Father protect His children, whose love and power are infinitely greater.
- Joseph sold into slavery: His brothers meant evil, but Joseph said, "God sent me" (Genesis 45:5-8; 50:20). What was undoubtedly a grievous sin became, by the time it reached Joseph, the greatest blessing of his life.
- The lady's vision at the prayer meeting: A woman struggled with second causes until she asked God to teach her the truth. She received a vision of advancing light -- the presence of God -- that enveloped everything: fighting armies, wicked men, raging beasts, storms, pestilence, sin, and suffering. She saw that not a lion could reach out its paw, nor a bullet fly, unless God's presence moved aside to permit it. Even petty annoyances -- a cross look, a harsh word -- were all enveloped. Her difficulty vanished forever. God was in everything.
- Elisha and the Syrian army (2 Kings 6): The servant saw the city surrounded by a great host and was terrified. Elisha said, "They that be with us are more than they that be with them." God opened the servant's eyes, and he saw the mountain full of horses and chariots of fire.
- Nancy, the joyous colored woman: When a gloomy Christian lady listed all the things that might go wrong ("suppose you get sick, suppose your employers leave..."), Nancy cried, "Stop! I never supposes. De Lord is my shepherd, and I knows I shall not want. It's all dem supposes as is makin' you so misable."
- Joseph's chariots: Being sold into slavery and cast into prison were the very chariots that bore Joseph to the place of his exaltation.
