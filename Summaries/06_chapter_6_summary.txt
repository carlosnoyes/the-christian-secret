Chapter 6 Summary: Difficulties Concerning Faith
=================================================

Main Idea
---------
Faith is not a mysterious religious feeling or a tangible spiritual possession; it is simply believing God, just as sight is simply seeing. Believers already exercise trust constantly in everyday life -- trusting cooks, train conductors, nurses, and strangers -- yet they claim they cannot trust God. The chapter argues that faith is the simplest thing in the world, and the real barrier is not a lack of ability to believe but an unwillingness to believe God's word.

Key Points
----------
- Faith is misunderstood as something tangible or mystical. In reality, it is nothing more than believing God when He says He has done or will do something, and then trusting Him to do it.
- The virtue of faith does not lie in the act of believing itself, but in the object believed. Believing truth saves; believing a lie destroys. Faith is merely the link to the Saviour.
- Everyone exercises enormous trust in daily life without a second thought -- trusting strangers with health, life, and precious children. It is irrational to trust fallible humans but refuse to trust God.
- Saying "I have no faith" really means "I have no faith in God." Completing the sentence reveals how dreadful the statement is.
- Trust and worry are utterly incompatible. If you truly trust God with something, you cease worrying about it. Worry is proof of a lack of trust.
- God's order is always: (1) the Fact, (2) the Faith, (3) the Feeling. Humans reverse this and demand feeling first, which keeps them in bondage.
- The will must be placed on the believing side. Say "I will believe, I do believe" and persist against every suggestion of doubt.

Illustrations and Examples
--------------------------
- The daily-life absurdity: Imagine going through a single day refusing to trust anyone -- not eating breakfast for fear the cook poisoned it, not riding a train for fear of the engineer, not believing a newspaper or a friend. The utter impossibility of such a day shows how natural trust is and how absurd it is to withhold it from God.
- Mothers and nurses: Mothers constantly trust their most precious infants to nurses without a shadow of anxiety, yet believers will not trust their souls to the Saviour who died for them.
- The emancipation of the slaves: The government proclaimed the slaves free. Their freedom was a fact from the moment of the Proclamation, but they only experienced it when they heard the news and believed it. One old woman in a Southern town remained in slavery for years after the war because she had heard the news but her master denied it and she was afraid to believe. When a Northern lady confirmed the truth, she believed, declared "I's free!" and walked out. The divine order -- Fact, Faith, Feeling -- is illustrated perfectly.
- The old volume of sermons: Early in her Christian life, the author was stirred by an appeal that those who loved the Lord should show others how worthy He was of trust by the steadfastness of their own faith.
