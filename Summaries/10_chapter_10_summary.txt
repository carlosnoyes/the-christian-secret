Chapter 10 Summary: Service
============================

Main Idea
---------
Christian service becomes a burden and a treadmill when driven by duty alone,
but when the believer fully surrenders their will to God, He works within them
to want to do His will, transforming service from bondage into perfect freedom
and delight.

Key Points
----------
- In lower forms of Christian life, service becomes wearisome duty; the soul
  says "Must I?" instead of "May I?" and the yoke begins to gall.
- God's plan under the new covenant is to write His laws on our hearts and
  minds so that we love and desire to obey them, not merely comply outwardly.
- When the will is surrendered to God, He works in us "to will and to do of
  His good pleasure," making hard things easy and bitter things sweet.
- The life of faith also delivers from anxiety about fitness and results; our
  utter helplessness is actually our greatest qualification, because God's
  strength is made perfect in weakness.
- God does not give general, blanket commands but leads step by step, giving
  specific guidance for each hour; believers must not turn one particular
  leading into a universal obligation.
- After-reflections on service -- whether self-congratulation or self-despair
  -- are equally harmful; we should refuse all self-reflective acts and leave
  results entirely with God.
- Trusting God with our work means not worrying over it; trust and worry
  cannot coexist.
- This does not make us puppets but servants; the responsibility for plans,
  materials, and results belongs to the Master, not the servant.

Illustrations and Examples
--------------------------
- The Christian woman who initially served joyfully but eventually envied
  servants scrubbing floors, finding her spiritual duties unbearable.
- Worldly men who gladly endure hardship for money or ambition, contrasted
  with Christians who lament far lesser sacrifices for Christ.
- Parents wishing they could get inside a child's will to make obedience easy
  -- illustrating God's new covenant strategy.
- The idiot asylum and the dumb-bell exercises: a little girl with no strength
  of her own made perfect movements because the master stood behind her and
  did it all -- her utter weakness was her greatest strength.
- The steam engine and machinery: disconnected, the machinery is useless, but
  once connected to the engine's power, it runs effortlessly.
- The old book maxim: "Never indulge, at the close of an action, in any
  self-reflective acts of any kind."
- The young Christian woman who thought she must speak to every person she met
  about their souls, falling into hopeless bondage until taught that God gives
  specific, not general, commands.
- The slave on a storm-tossed ship who whistled contentedly because any loss
  would be his master's, not his own.
