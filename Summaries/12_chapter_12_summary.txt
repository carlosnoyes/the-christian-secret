Chapter 12 Summary: Concerning Temptation
==========================================

Main Idea
---------
Temptation is not sin, and entering the life of faith does not mean freedom
from temptation but rather increased spiritual warfare. Believers must learn
to distinguish between being tempted and actually sinning, refuse all
discouragement, and hand every temptation over to the Lord by faith.

Key Points
----------
- A common mistake is expecting that the life of faith will bring freedom from
  temptation itself; in reality, temptations generally increase tenfold after
  entering the interior life.
- Strong temptations are a sign of great grace, not little grace, just as the
  Israelites faced far more enemies inside the Promised Land than in the
  wilderness.
- Temptation is not sin; it becomes sin only when we stop, entertain it, and
  give a half-consent of the will to it. Hearing evil suggestions is no more
  sinful than hearing a wicked man's talk on the street.
- The enemy's chief strategy is to make believers think that the temptation
  itself is their own sin, plunging them into condemnation and discouragement,
  which then leads to actual sin.
- Discouragement is one of the most fatal things in the life of faith;
  cheerfulness is one of the most helpful. "All discouragement is from the
  devil."
- What appears to be righteous self-disgust at being tempted is often wounded
  self-love and secret self-congratulation exposed; true humility drives the
  soul to deeper trust rather than faithless discouragement.
- Time spent combating temptation is not wasted; we are fighting the Lord's
  battles, and endurance of temptation develops patience, strength, and
  dependence on God.
- Temptation is God's instrument for completing our perfection; the enemy's
  own weapons are turned against him as all things work together for good.
- Victory over temptation comes by faith alone: committing the temptation to
  Christ and leaving it with Him, just as we committed ourselves to Him for
  salvation.

Illustrations and Examples
--------------------------
- The Canaanites still in the land: seven great nations, thirty-one kings,
  walled cities, and giants awaited Israel inside the Promised Land, far more
  than they faced in the wilderness.
- The burglar analogy: a thief breaks into a house and, when resisted, accuses
  the homeowner of being the thief -- illustrating how Satan attributes his
  own suggestions to the believer.
- The allegory of Satan's council: evil spirits propose to make a good man sin
  through pleasures of sin and pains of virtue, but Satan rejects both; only
  the imp who says "I will discourage his soul" wins Satan's approval.
- The lady living a sheltered life who was horrified by blasphemous thoughts
  during prayer, not realizing they were the enemy's suggestions; once she
  recognized their source and refused them, she was delivered.
- The saint of the desert who asked, "Where wert thou, Lord, while I was being
  tempted?" and received the answer, "Close beside thee, my son, all the
  while."
- The invalid lady and the butterfly cocoon: she clipped the cocoon open to
  help the struggling butterfly, but the wings came out lifeless and colorless
  because the struggle was needed to send life-fluid into the veins -- just as
  spiritual struggle with temptation strengthens the soul's wings.
