Chapter 21 Summary: 'Without Me Ye Can Do Nothing'
====================================================

Main Idea
---------
The chapter argues that the common divorce between "spiritual" life and "temporal"
life is one of the greatest hindrances to real union with God. Since Christ
declared that apart from Him we can do literally nothing, every act of daily
life -- housekeeping, business, social duties -- must be done in dependence on Him
and for His glory, making all of life a sacrament.

Key Points
----------
- A major cause of spiritual fruitlessness is the unnatural separation between
  religious life and everyday temporal life, as though religion were an outside
  garment put on for Sundays and taken off on Mondays.
- Jesus' words "Without me ye can do nothing" mean that any living or doing
  apart from Him amounts to "nothing" in God's sight.
- If we are truly one with Christ, independent action is impossible in the very
  nature of things, just as a branch severed from the vine withers and dies.
- If Christ works in the Christian who leads prayer meetings, then Christ must
  also work in the Christian who keeps house, makes bargains, and darns
  stockings; one duty is as religious as the other.
- Recognizing God's presence in all temporal activities transforms life: the
  kitchen, workshop, nursery, and parlor become as sacred as the prayer meeting.
- It is vain to "rise up early, sit up late, and eat the bread of sorrows"
  when God has assumed the care and responsibility of our work; we are to
  enter into His rest and cease from our own labored self-effort.
- The change required is not so much in outward acts as in motive and
  dependence -- doing the same things but acknowledging God in them all.
- This practice of doing everything for God brings divine wisdom, greater
  efficiency, and an abiding sense of Christ's presence.

Illustrations and Examples
--------------------------
- A conversation with a Quaker preacher while driving through Philadelphia
  Park: the preacher used a natural tone in conversation but felt he could not
  do so in the pulpit because it was "more solemn," illustrating the artificial
  divide between sacred and secular life.
- A vine and its branches: a branch that severs its connection with the vine
  for even part of its growing will wither and die in that part.
- A young Christian mother overwhelmed by household duties who felt separated
  from God all day; when told to see God in her homely duties, her life became
  "a transformed and glorified thing."
- A young lady in a fashionable family who felt paying social calls separated
  her from God; when told to pay calls for God's glory, she exclaimed, "Pay
  calls for God! I never heard of such a thing," then saw that nothing but sin
  need separate her from Him.
- A mother rising early and sitting up late to mend her child's torn clothes
  so the child can rest -- illustrating how God bears our burdens so we can
  enter His rest.
- Scientists seeking to resolve all forces into one primal force, paralleled
  with the Bible's teaching that God-force is behind everything.
