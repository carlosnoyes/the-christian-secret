Chapter 14 Summary: Doubts
===========================

**Main Idea**:
Doubting is not an inevitable trial to be endured but a sinful habit to be conquered. Just as a drunkard must take a total abstinence pledge against alcohol, believers must make a definite, permanent surrender of their liberty to doubt and trust God completely. Deliverance from doubt comes through the same means as deliverance from any other sin -- by handing it over to the Lord.

**Key Points**:
- Many Christians are enslaved to habitual doubting, which wrecks their usefulness, joy, and communion with God.
- Even believers who have entered the life of faith may shift their doubting to a "higher platform," accepting God's forgiveness but doubting His promises and declarations.
- Doubting is not a spiritual conflict but a spiritual rebellion; the fight of faith ends the moment doubt begins.
- Doubting is actually a luxury that people secretly enjoy -- it provides a perverse comfort of self-pity and sympathy, much like indulging in hard thoughts against others.
- Doubts often disguise themselves as humility ("I am too wicked for God to care for"), but this is really indulging in hard thoughts about God, since He came to save sinners, not the righteous.
- The Bible everywhere commands trust and treats doubt as sin and provocation against God.
- Deliverance from doubt requires a definite, total-abstinence-style pledge -- not a gradual reduction but a complete, once-for-all surrender of the will to doubt.
- When doubts assault, the believer must immediately lift the shield of faith, refuse to argue with the doubts, and repeat simple affirmations of trust such as "Jesus saves me."
- The will is the stronghold; once the will to doubt is surrendered to God, His Spirit works to keep the believer from falling.

**Illustrations and Examples**:
- Christian's imprisonment in Doubting Castle by Giant Despair (from Bunyan's Pilgrim's Progress): used as a universally recognized picture of the believer's experience with doubt.
- The two little girls left at the author's house: one played happily with childlike trust while her mother was away; the other sat in a corner, convincing herself her mother would forget her and had abandoned her -- the mother's indignant, grief-stricken response upon return powerfully illustrated how God feels about our doubts.
- The lost lamb and the sick man: a lamb saying the shepherd does not love it because it wandered, or a sick man saying the doctor will not visit because he is ill, show the absurdity of doubting God's care because of our unworthiness.
- The drunkard analogy: just as a drunkard must take a total abstinence pledge, so the doubter must make a definite transaction of surrender, giving up the liberty to doubt forever.
- The author's personal morning battle: she describes waking to an army of doubts clamoring for admittance, and defeating them by immediately affirming "The Lord does love me... Jesus saves me now."
- The author's daughter Ray: as a young child, she fought two battles with Satan's doubts -- first refusing to disbelieve the Bible ("Satan, I will believe it. So there!") and then refusing to accept she was too naughty for Jesus to love ("Satan, shut thy mouth!") -- described as a grander battle than any soul ever fought.
