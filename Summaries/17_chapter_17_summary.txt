Chapter 17 Summary: Oneness with Christ
========================================

Main Idea
---------
The chapter teaches that true oneness with Christ is not measured by emotional
experiences or ecstatic feelings, but by a Christ-like character, life, and walk.
God's ultimate purpose for every believer is soul-union with Himself, a mystery
planned before the foundation of the world and accomplished through the
incarnation of Christ.

Key Points
----------
- Oneness with Christ is the final destination of all previous steps in the
  Christian life; God has made us for it and our hearts cannot find rest until
  we embrace it.
- The disciples illustrate the usual course of Christian experience: first
  allegiance to Christ as an outward Lord, then Pentecost brought inward union
  where Christ became their indwelling Life.
- True oneness consists in Christ-like character, not in feelings or emotions.
  If there is no likeness of character, unity of aim, and similarity of thought
  and action, there can be no real oneness.
- Emotions are unreliable indicators of spiritual states; they depend largely
  on temperament and physical conditions and should not be used as a test of
  union with Christ.
- The test of oneness is doing the same works as Christ, just as Jesus proved
  His oneness with the Father by doing the Father's works.
- Oneness with Christ is already the possession of every believer, even the
  weakest; it is not something new to obtain, but something to realize.
- Three practical steps to realize this union: (1) be convinced from Scripture
  of God's indwelling, (2) surrender your whole being to Him, and (3) believe
  He has taken possession and is dwelling in you.

Illustrations and Examples
--------------------------
- The history of the disciples before and after Pentecost as a picture of the
  stages of Christian experience.
- The analogy of Christ living in a house, shut up in a far-off closet,
  unknown to the household, longing to be known and invited into every room.
- A colored servant who became unconscious at revival meetings with powerful
  emotional experiences, yet showed hardly any spiritual life at other times.
- A colored congregation known for stealing, lying, and cheating, yet whose
  nightly meetings were times of great emotion; their preacher refused to
  preach on morality because "it always brings a coldness over de meetings."
- The analogy of two friends: no matter how much affection is expressed, if
  aims and actions are opposite, there is no real oneness.
