Chapter 7 Summary: Difficulties Concerning the Will
====================================================

Main Idea
---------
The life of faith is not lived in the emotions but in the will. The will is the true governing power of a person -- the "king" of one's nature -- and when the will is surrendered to God, the rest of the nature must eventually follow. Many believers feel like hypocrites because their emotions rebel against what they have chosen to believe, but the chapter teaches that the decisions of the will are the real measure of the soul's condition, not the clamoring of feelings.

Key Points
----------
- Fenelon's principle: "Pure religion resides in the will alone." The will is the central self, the "Ego," the deciding power to which all else must yield.
- Emotions are servants, not the king. They belong to us and are experienced by us, but they are not ourselves. God seeks possession of the will, not the feelings.
- When the will is set on God's side, the transaction is as real in God's sight as if every emotion agreed, even though it may not feel real to the believer.
- Feelings of unreality or hypocrisy should not be heeded. They are only in the emotions and are not worth a moment's thought if the will is firmly placed in God's hands.
- The biblical word "heart" refers not to emotions but to the will -- the personality, the central self -- and God's dealings aim at bringing this "I" under His control.
- The Holy Spirit's work is assumed and relied upon throughout. The author emphasizes man's part (the will) precisely because the Spirit's power is always available to accomplish what the will consents to.

Illustrations and Examples
--------------------------
- The doubting young man: A young man of great intelligence was enslaved by habitual doubting. Nothing seemed true or real to his emotions. He was told to put his will on the believing side and say "I will believe, I do believe," regardless of his emotions. He did so, and after a few days of steadfastly holding to his will's decision against the clamoring of his feelings, the Holy Spirit brought every emotion and thought into captivity. The result was one of the grandest Christian lives the author knew of.
- The lady facing a great trial: Every emotion rose up in rebellion against a prospective trial. She chose to ignore her emotions and repeat "Thy will be done!" over and over. In an incredibly short time, even her emotions began to rejoice in God's will.
- The lady with a besetting sin: She loved a particular sin in her emotions but hated it in her will. She put her will on God's side, saying "I will not do it any more," and God took possession of her will and delivered her from the sin's power.
- The mother in the nursery: The will is like a wise mother; the feelings are like clamoring children. If the mother holds her course calmly, the children eventually yield. If she lets the children rule, there is nothing but confusion.
- The letter of William Hill (1842): A Presbyterian minister's account of his own experience. He made a covenant of consecration intellectually, with no change in his feelings -- "a heart full of hardness and darkness, unbelief and sin." He held to his will's decision, learned to live moment by moment in dependence on Christ's grace, and found steady victory over sins that had formerly enslaved him. He covenanted to walk by faith, not by feelings.
