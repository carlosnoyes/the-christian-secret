Chapter 10: Service — Discussion Questions
============================================

1. Smith describes a woman whose Christian service had become "an unspeakable burden" — she envied servants scrubbing floors because at least that was honest labor, not the treadmill of spiritual performance. Have you ever experienced Christian work shifting from "May I?" to "Must I?" — and what caused that shift?

2. The chapter's central claim is that God's plan is not to command us from the outside but to get inside us and make us *want* to do His will, so that obedience becomes "perfect freedom." How is this different from mere self-motivation or positive thinking — and what does it actually feel like when God changes what you want?

3. Smith tells of a girl in an institution who made perfect dumb-bell movements — not because she had skill, but because she had no strength at all, and the master stood behind her doing everything. Smith says "her utter weakness was her greatest strength." How does this picture challenge the way most churches identify, train, and celebrate their leaders and workers?

4. The chapter warns against two kinds of "self-reflective acts" after service: self-congratulation and self-despair. Smith says we should refuse both and simply leave the results with God. In practice, how do you debrief or learn from ministry experiences without falling into either trap — and is there a form of honest evaluation that avoids both pride and discouragement?

5. Smith says many Christians mistake a specific leading (speak to this person today) for a universal command (speak to every person always), and end up in hopeless bondage. How do you discern the difference between a personal, momentary leading of the Spirit and a general principle you should always follow?
