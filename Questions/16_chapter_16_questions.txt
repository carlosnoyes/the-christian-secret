Chapter 16: The Joy of Obedience — Discussion Questions
========================================================

1. Smith opens with the sentence "Perfect obedience would be perfect happiness, if only we had perfect confidence in the power we were obeying." What would need to change in your understanding of God — not just intellectually but in your gut — for total obedience to feel like total joy rather than total loss?

2. The chapter distinguishes sharply between friendship and love: friendship allows separate interests and moderate devotion, but love demands total union where "all that belongs to one becomes the property of the other." Smith says God offers us not friendship but love. How does this reframe the seemingly extreme demands of the Christian life — and does viewing obedience as the response of a lover rather than the duty of a servant change your posture toward it?

3. Smith says some believers are called to a narrower, more costly path than others, and rather than resenting this, they should see it as a sign of greater intimacy with Christ. How do you respond to the idea that God asks more of some people than others — is this a comforting sign of closeness, or does it feel unfair?

4. Smith imagines watching a poor woman at her washtub and says that even this life, "lived in Christ and with Christ, following Him whithersoever He may lead, would be filled with an enthusiasm that would make every hour of it glorious." Is this romantic idealism, or does the chapter's logic genuinely support the claim that any life — no matter how mundane — can be transformed by obedient love?

5. The chapter asks: "Wilt thou be content with pleasing me and me only?" Most of us draw our sense of identity and worth from multiple sources — career, relationships, approval, accomplishments. What would it look like to genuinely have God's pleasure as your single measuring stick, and what would you have to release to get there?
