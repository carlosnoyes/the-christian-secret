Chapter 5: Difficulties Concerning Consecration — Discussion Questions
=======================================================================

1. Smith says the "one grand temptation" at the point of consecration is waiting to feel consecrated before believing it has happened. Why do you think we are so reluctant to trust a transaction we cannot feel — and what other important commitments in life (marriage vows, legal contracts, forgiveness) do we accept as real before the feelings catch up?

2. The chapter uses the vivid illustration of giving an estate to a friend, then returning day after day to re-give it because you are not sure the gift was real. Smith says many Christians do exactly this with God. What drives this compulsive re-surrendering — is it genuine humility, hidden distrust, or something else — and what would it take to simply leave the gift on the altar and walk away?

3. Smith argues that "it was not the intention of the giver that made it holy, but the holiness of the receiver" — that the altar sanctifies the gift. How does this reframe the pressure many Christians feel to get their motives perfectly pure before they can approach God? And is there a danger in relying too heavily on this idea?

4. The chapter gives a striking rule: "Never, under any circumstances, give way for one single moment to doubt or discouragement," because "all discouragement is from the devil." Do you find this counsel liberating or potentially harmful — what happens when a person treats every doubt as demonic and refuses to sit with legitimate spiritual questions?

5. Smith insists you must "pay no attention to your feelings as a test of your relations with God" and attend only to the state of your will and faith. In a culture that increasingly values emotional awareness and processing feelings, how do you hold together Smith's counsel with the reality that emotions often carry important information about our spiritual condition?
