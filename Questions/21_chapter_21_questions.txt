Chapter 21: 'Without Me Ye Can Do Nothing' — Discussion Questions
===================================================================

1. Smith says the "wide divorce between things spiritual and things temporal" is one of the greatest hindrances to real union with God. Most of us unconsciously split life into sacred and secular — prayer versus housework, church versus business. Where do you draw that line in your own life, and what would collapse if you erased it entirely?

2. The young mother who felt "separated from God all day long" because her children consumed every moment was transformed when she saw those homely duties as service to the Lord. Have you experienced a similar sense of being cut off from God by the demands of ordinary life — and what shifted (or could shift) to make those very demands the place where you meet Him?

3. The young socialite exclaimed, "Pay calls for God! I never heard of such a thing." Smith's reply was that if it is right to do at all, it must be done for God's glory, and if it cannot be done for Him, it should not be done at all. How would applying this simple test — "Can I do this for God?" — change the way you evaluate your daily activities, social obligations, and leisure?

4. Smith argues that bringing God into our everyday affairs will not only transform our experience of them but will actually make us more competent — "the house will be kept more nicely, the children trained more swiftly, the bargain made more satisfactorily." Is this a fair promise, or does it risk turning God into a productivity tool? What is the difference between depending on God for the doing of our work and using God to get better results?

5. An old spiritual writer quoted in the chapter says that "to become a saint it is not always necessary to change our works, but only to put an interior purpose towards God in them all." This means your path to the deepest spiritual life may not require dramatic changes but simply a new orientation within the life you already have. Does this surprise you — and does it make sanctification feel more accessible or more challenging?
