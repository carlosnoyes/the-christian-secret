Chapter 1: God's Side and Man's Side — Discussion Questions
============================================================

1. Smith argues that the clay's only job is to "lie passive" in the potter's hands. In practice, what does spiritual passivity actually look like — and how do you distinguish genuine surrender from laziness, resignation, or avoidance of responsibility?

2. The chapter claims that all efforts at self-transformation result in "the marring of the vessel." Yet most of us have been shaped by disciplines we chose — education, therapy, habit-building. Where, if anywhere, do you draw the line between healthy human effort and the self-effort Smith warns against?

3. Smith uses the image of a June apple that is "perfect" for June, even though it is far from an October apple. How does this idea sit with you when applied to real people — especially when someone's "June stage" involves patterns that genuinely hurt others? Can we call something perfect and incomplete at the same time?

4. The two preachers in the opening illustration each taught one side of the truth and produced confusion in their hearers. In your own experience, which side — God's sovereign work or human responsibility — have you tended to over-emphasize, and what has that imbalance cost you?

5. Smith tells of a woman who accepted God's mundane training through sweeping and baking and was transformed, while another woman refused similar training and "went back into coldness and misery." What makes the difference between a person who can receive unwelcome circumstances as formative and one who resists them — and is that difference itself something we produce or something God gives?
