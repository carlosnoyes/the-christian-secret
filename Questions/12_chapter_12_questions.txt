Chapter 12: Concerning Temptation — Discussion Questions
=========================================================

1. Smith says that temptations generally *increase* tenfold after entering the deeper life of faith, and that strong temptations are "a sign of great grace, rather than of little grace." How does this reframe the way you interpret seasons of intense spiritual struggle — and does it change how you would counsel someone who feels overwhelmed by temptation?

2. The chapter draws a sharp line between temptation and sin: hearing the enemy's whisper is no more sinful than hearing a wicked man swear as you walk past. Yet many Christians feel polluted by the mere presence of a dark thought. What makes it so hard to accept that being tempted is not the same as sinning — and what practical difference would it make in your daily life to truly believe this?

3. Smith says the enemy's chief strategy is discouragement — that "all discouragement is from the devil." In the allegory, Satan's council tried pleasure and pain first, but it was the imp who said "I will discourage his soul" that won the day. How do you distinguish between godly conviction (which leads to repentance and hope) and the enemy's discouragement (which leads to despair and paralysis)?

4. The butterfly cocoon story is striking: an act of kindness — cutting the struggle short — produced lifeless wings. Smith applies this to temptation: the struggle itself sends "life fluid" into our spiritual wings. Can you identify a time when a struggle you wished God would remove actually produced something essential in your character that ease never could have?

5. Smith acknowledges that some temptations feel so personal and horrifying that we assume they must reveal something rotten in us. The lady who was assaulted by dreadful thoughts during prayer concluded she had never even been born again. How do you handle the shame that comes with being tempted in ways that feel beneath you — and what would it look like to treat such moments as the enemy's attack rather than your own failure?
