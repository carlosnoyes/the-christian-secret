Chapter 4: How to Enter In — Discussion Questions
===================================================

1. Smith says this life of faith is "not an attainment but an obtainment" — a gift, not an achievement. Yet she also says it requires two definite steps: entire consecration and absolute faith. How do you reconcile the idea that something is purely a gift with the reality that it demands such deliberate, costly action on your part?

2. The story of little Charley's mother reveals that she was terrified to say "Thy will be done" because she imagined God would use His authority to make her miserable. Smith says many Christians share this fear. What experiences, teachings, or assumptions have shaped your own instinctive picture of what God would do if you gave Him truly unrestricted access to your life?

3. Smith compares consecration to a patient placing his whole case in a doctor's hands "without any reserves" and obeying implicitly. In an age that rightly values personal autonomy, informed consent, and questioning authority, how do you think about this call to unconditional surrender — is it wisdom, or does it carry risks of spiritual passivity or even abuse?

4. The chapter insists that faith must be in the present tense — "My sins are now forgiven," "I am now delivered" — and that future-tense faith accomplishes nothing. Why do you think it is so much easier to believe God will act someday than to believe He is acting right now, and what would change in your daily life if you genuinely held a present-tense faith?

5. The man at the bottom of the well lets go of the rope in terror, expecting to fall thirty feet, and drops only three inches. What is the "rope" you are clinging to — the thing you are afraid to release — and what do you think you would actually find if you let go?
