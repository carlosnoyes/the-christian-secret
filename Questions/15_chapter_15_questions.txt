Chapter 15: Practical Results in the Daily Walk and Conversation — Discussion Questions
========================================================================================

1. Smith's son wrote that "the world will not read the Bible, but they will read our lives." The Hindu visitor concluded that Christians need "a little more of Christ's Christianity, and a little less of man's." If an honest, thoughtful outsider observed your daily life for a week, what would they conclude about the God you claim to follow?

2. The chapter lists specific marks of the life of faith: meekness, quietness of spirit, sweetness under provocation, calmness amid turmoil, insensibility to slights, and freedom from worry. Smith presents these not as rare achievements but as "the natural outward development" of the inward life. Which of these marks feels most foreign to your current experience, and what does that gap reveal?

3. Smith draws a sharp distinction between "man's Christianity" which teaches sacrifice to save ourselves, and "Christ's Christianity" which teaches sacrifice to save others. Where do you see this self-serving version of Christianity operating in your own life or in the church more broadly — and what would it cost to move toward the other kind?

4. The chapter says the standard of practical holy living has been "so low among Christians that any good degree of real devotedness is looked upon with surprise, and even often with disapprobation." Why do you think the church tends to normalize mediocre spiritual living — and what would happen in your community if someone actually lived the way Smith describes?

5. Smith insists that yielding in "little things" — habits, dispositions, small acts of obedience — reaches the pliability God needs more quickly than the great dramatic surrenders. What small, seemingly insignificant area of your life might God be speaking to you about right now that you have been dismissing as "not worthy of the Lord's attention"?
