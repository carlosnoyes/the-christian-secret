Chapter 3: The Life Defined — Discussion Questions
====================================================

1. Smith says the greatest burden we carry in life is "self" — our temperaments, weaknesses, feelings, and inward struggles — and that this must be the first thing surrendered to God. What does it actually look like in daily practice to "hand yourself over" rather than manage yourself, and how do you tell the difference between genuine self-surrender and neglecting legitimate self-care?

2. Hannah, the poor woman in the story, says most people take their burdens to God but then "bring them away with them again." Why do you think it is so difficult to leave a burden with God once you have brought it to Him — what is it about our nature or our theology that pulls us back into carrying what we have already handed over?

3. Smith uses the analogy of a child in a wealthy father's house — carefree, provided for, taking no thought for the morrow. Yet most adults would say a certain amount of planning and worry is just responsible living. Where is the line between the "anxious thought" Jesus forbids and the prudent forethought that wisdom seems to require?

4. The chapter insists that "Be careful for nothing" covers everything — both spiritual and temporal, inward and outward — with no exceptions. Smith anticipates the objection that some things (children, church, business) surely deserve our anxiety. How do you respond to that absolute claim — is there truly nothing a Christian should be anxious about, or does Smith's framework need qualification?

5. Smith compares resting in God to the delicious sensation of lying down on a bed after an exhausting day — but then asks what would happen if you doubted the bed would hold you. In your experience, what are the specific things that make the "bed" of God's faithfulness feel unstable, and what has helped you (or could help you) actually relax into trust rather than straining to hold yourself up?
