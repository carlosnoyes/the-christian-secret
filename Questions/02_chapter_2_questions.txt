Chapter 2: The Scripturalness of This Life — Discussion Questions
==================================================================

1. Smith paints a vivid picture of the believer's common experience: "one hour sinning, and the next repenting; and beginning again, only to fail again." Most Christians would recognize this cycle. Do you think we have quietly accepted this pattern as normal — and if so, what would it cost us emotionally and theologically to genuinely believe that something better is available?

2. The chapter uses the analogy of a redeemed captive who is still forced to "wear his fetters" and asks, "Would not any one think himself cheated?" How do you respond to that challenge — is it fair to say that a salvation which leaves sin's power intact would be a kind of fraud, or does that overstate what redemption promises in this life?

3. Smith marshals passage after passage to argue that Christ's death was meant to deliver us not just from the guilt of sin but from its power and dominion. Yet many sincere, Bible-reading Christians live as though ongoing defeat is inevitable. What accounts for that gap between what Scripture seems to promise and what most believers actually experience?

4. The chapter quotes a seventeenth-century divine who asks whether it is "inconsistent with true faith" to believe that Christ, to whom all power is given, will "suffer sin and the devil to have dominion" over believers. Where do you land on this — is persistent sin in a believer's life primarily a failure of faith, a misunderstanding of doctrine, or something else entirely?

5. Smith closes by comparing God to the maker of a machine: if the machine cannot accomplish its declared purpose, we would call the maker "a fraud." She then says we must not dare think God's creature is incapable of fulfilling its created purpose. Does this argument compel you, or does it gloss over the real complexity of human freedom, suffering, and the "not yet" dimension of redemption?
