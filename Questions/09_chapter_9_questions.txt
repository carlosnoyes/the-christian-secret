Chapter 9: Growth — Discussion Questions
==========================================

1. Smith tells of a woman who had been "growing in grace" for twenty-five years yet was less devoted than at the start. Smith's diagnosis is that she was trying to grow *into* grace rather than *in* it. What is the practical difference between these two — and how would you recognize in your own life whether your spiritual effort is producing genuine growth or just exhausting motion?

2. The chapter insists that true spiritual growth happens the way a lily grows — without toil, strain, or even consciousness of growing — simply by receiving sun and rain. Yet most of us have been formed by disciplines that required real effort (prayer routines, Scripture study, accountability). How do you reconcile Smith's "effortless growth" with the genuine role of spiritual discipline?

3. Smith says the believer must be content to "grow a potato as gladly as a rose" if that is God's will — cultivating homely everyday virtues as willingly as exalted spiritual experiences. What does this say about our tendency to value dramatic spiritual breakthroughs over the slow, unglamorous work of becoming patient, kind, or faithful in small things?

4. The chapter compares many Christians to a child who believes he must use "a combination of rope and pulleys" to stretch himself to the desired height. Where in your spiritual life have you been pulling on ropes — expending enormous effort on growth that only God can produce — and what would it look like to stop straining and simply abide?

5. Smith promises that God can transform "the very things that were before our greatest hindrances into the chiefest and most blessed means of our growth" — right where we are, without transplanting us to different circumstances. Can you identify a current hindrance in your life that might actually be the soil God has chosen for your growth, and what would change if you believed that?
