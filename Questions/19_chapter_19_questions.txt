Chapter 19: Kings and Their Kingdoms — Discussion Questions
============================================================

1. Smith says the kingdom of God is entirely interior — "not with observation" — and that its kings reign not through outward power but through inward qualities like meekness, service, and self-sacrifice. How does this invert the way our culture (and often our churches) define leadership and success, and what would it look like to actually organize a community around this principle?

2. The story of the monk of St. Cyr is remarkable: persecuted, humiliated, forced to carry a noisome burden and beg with a placard declaring him "the vilest of the vile" — yet he conquered every enemy through unfailing kindness and love. When the violent criminal was locked in his cell, it was meekness, not force, that subdued him. Is this kind of response humanly possible, or does it require something superhuman — and have you ever witnessed anything close to it?

3. Smith contrasts Ulysses, who escaped the sirens' song by plugging his ears with wax and binding himself to the mast, with Orpheus, who sang a sweeter song and sailed past with disdain. She says God's kingdom works like Orpheus — not by outward restraint but by inward delight that makes the temptation irrelevant. What "sweeter song" has made a particular sin or worldly attachment lose its power over you?

4. Christ says, "Except ye become as little children, ye shall not enter the kingdom of heaven." Smith defines childlikeness as simplicity, trustfulness, light-hearted carelessness, and unquestioning obedience. Which of these qualities do you find hardest to practice as an adult, and what specific thing would you have to lay down to recover it?

5. Smith says Joseph "reigned a king in slavery and in prison as truly as when seated on Pharaoh's throne." She argues that true kingship means being inwardly free regardless of outward circumstances. Where in your current life do your circumstances feel like they are reigning over you rather than the other way around — and what would it mean to sit on an inward throne in the midst of that situation?
