Chapter 18: 'Although' and 'Yet' — Discussion Questions
========================================================

1. Habakkuk's declaration — "Although the fig-tree shall not blossom... yet I will rejoice in the Lord" — is a commitment to joy in God when every visible support has failed. Have you ever been in an "although" season where nothing seemed to be working, and if so, were you able to reach the "yet"? What made the difference?

2. Smith says the soul must learn to trust "the bare God" — God stripped of His gifts, His promises, His blessings, His comforting presence — simply because of who He is. What is the difference between trusting God because of what He does for you and trusting God simply because He is God, and which kind of trust do you actually have?

3. The chapter describes three regions of the spiritual life: a region of beginnings full of sensible joys, a vast wilderness of dryness and darkness, and finally mountain heights of uninterrupted union with God. If you can locate yourself in one of these regions, what has that season been teaching you — and does knowing this framework change how you interpret your current experience?

4. Smith compares the self-life to a caterpillar that must die so the butterfly can emerge, saying God has "destined us for a higher life than the self-life" and therefore appointed the self-life to die. What does the death of self actually feel like in daily experience — and how do you distinguish between the painful but redemptive dying Smith describes and depression, burnout, or spiritual illness?

5. Smith says "it is grand to trust in the promises, but it is grander still to trust in the Promiser," because promises can be misunderstood or seem to fail, but the Person behind them never can. When has a promise of God seemed to fail you, and what did that experience teach you about the difference between clinging to a formula and clinging to a Person?
