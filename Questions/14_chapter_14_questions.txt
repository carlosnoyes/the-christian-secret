Chapter 14: Doubts — Discussion Questions
==========================================

1. Smith compares the habit of doubting to alcoholism and insists it requires a total-abstinence pledge, not moderation. She says the liberty to doubt must be "given up forever." Is this a healthy way to approach intellectual and spiritual uncertainty — and how do you distinguish between the sinful doubting Smith describes and the honest questioning that can lead to deeper faith?

2. The story of the two little girls left at Smith's house is vivid: one played happily, the other sat in a corner convinced her mother had forgotten her. The mother's reaction upon returning was grief, wounded love, and indignation. Smith says God responds the same way to our doubts. Does this picture of God's response to doubt comfort you or unsettle you — and what does it reveal about how God views the relationship between trust and love?

3. Smith argues that doubting is often "a real luxury" — that doubts come as "a company of sympathizing friends" and that entertaining them provides a strange fascination, much like nursing a grudge. Have you experienced this — the almost pleasurable quality of wallowing in doubt — and if so, what makes it so seductive?

4. The chapter says that doubting our own worthiness can masquerade as humility but is actually "indulging in hard and wrong thoughts" about God, since He said He came to save sinners, not the righteous. How do you tell the difference between genuine humility about your own weakness and the kind of self-focused unworthiness that is really a subtle form of unbelief?

5. Little Ray's response to Satan's doubts — "Satan, I will believe it. So there!" and "Satan, shut thy mouth!" — delighted her mother. Smith presents a child's blunt, uncomplicated refusal to entertain doubt as the model for adult faith. What would it look like for you, with all your adult complexity and sophistication, to respond to doubt with that kind of fierce simplicity?
