Chapter 20: The Chariots of God — Discussion Questions
=======================================================

1. Smith says God's chariots do not look like chariots — they look like "enemies, sufferings, trials, defeats, misunderstandings, disappointments, unkindnesses." The only difference between a Juggernaut car that crushes you and a chariot that carries you is whether you climb up into it or lie down under it. What current trial in your life might actually be a chariot waiting to carry you somewhere you have been praying to go?

2. The lady with the slow servant had been praying for patience for a long time before she realized the slow servant *was* God's answer to her prayer — the very chariot sent to carry her into patience. Have you ever recognized, in hindsight, that the thing you resented most was actually the instrument God was using to produce the very quality you were asking Him for?

3. Smith says God often has to destroy our own chariots — our favorite instrumentalities, our trusted spiritual supports, our dependence on a particular friend or ministry — before we will mount into His. She frames these losses not as punishments but as necessary removals of lesser vehicles. What "chariot of your own" has God removed or is currently removing, and can you see how that loss might be clearing the way for something better?

4. The chapter insists that by the time any trial reaches us — regardless of whether it was caused by men, devils, or circumstances — "it is God's chariot for you." This echoes Chapter 8's teaching about second causes. How do you hold this truth alongside the reality that some suffering is genuinely unjust and that the people who caused it bear real moral responsibility?

5. Smith says the way to mount into God's chariot is simply to find God in the trial and hide yourself in Him — like a baby in its mother's arms who "rides triumphantly through the hardest places and does not even know they are hard." What would it practically look like for you, this week, to stop analyzing your difficulty and instead climb into it as God's chariot by saying "Thy will be done" and resting in His presence within it?
