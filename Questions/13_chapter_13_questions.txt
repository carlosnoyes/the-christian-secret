Chapter 13: Failures — Discussion Questions
=============================================

1. Smith says the proper response to failure is not discouragement but an instant return to God — confessing the sin, believing His forgiveness on the spot, and trusting more fully than before. The little girl in the story thought God should "make us feel sorry for two or three days first." Which response is more natural to you, and what does your instinct reveal about your picture of God?

2. The chapter contrasts two people who sinned after entering the life of faith: the man who spent three miserable years in unbelief before someone showed him 1 John 1:9, and the woman who confessed while still boiling with anger and was restored in five minutes. What made the difference between them — and what keeps us from taking the short path back to God?

3. Smith uses the story of Israel's defeat at Ai to argue that the cause of spiritual failure is always a hidden "accursed thing" — some secret, un-surrendered corner of the heart — not the strength of the enemy. She then tells of a sealed cask in her cellar that bred moths throughout her house for years. What "sealed casks" might be sitting undisturbed in your own life, and what makes you reluctant to open them?

4. The chapter warns that our suffering over our failures can itself become a deeper sin than the original fault — because it is really wounded self-love, not genuine repentance, that drives it. How do you tell the difference between godly sorrow that leads to restoration and the self-focused chagrin that keeps you wallowing in failure?

5. Smith insists that sanctification is "not a thing to be picked up at a certain stage and forever after possessed, but a life to be lived day by day and hour by hour." How does viewing holiness as a *walk* rather than a *state* change the way you handle your own inconsistency — and does it make the Christian life feel more hopeful or more demanding?
