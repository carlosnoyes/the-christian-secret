Chapter 6: Difficulties Concerning Faith — Discussion Questions
================================================================

1. Smith argues that faith is "nothing more nor less than just believing God" — as plain and natural as sight is seeing. She then asks why we trust cooks, train conductors, and nurses without a second thought but claim we cannot trust God. What is it about our relationship with God that makes trust feel so much harder than the trust we extend to strangers every day?

2. The chapter establishes a divine order — Fact, Faith, Feeling — and says that most people reverse it, waiting to feel something before they will believe. Where in your own spiritual life have you been waiting for the feeling before you were willing to exercise faith, and what would it look like to act on the fact regardless?

3. The story of the emancipated slaves who remained in bondage years after the Proclamation because their master denied the news is striking. Smith suggests many Christians live in a similar condition — legally free but experientially enslaved. What are the "old masters" (voices, habits, beliefs) that keep telling you the good news is not really true, and how do they maintain their power?

4. Smith makes the provocative claim that trust and worry are "more utterly incompatible than even oil and water" — that if you are worrying about something, you are by definition not trusting God with it. Is this a helpful diagnostic or an unrealistic standard? Can a person genuinely trust God and still experience anxiety?

5. Near the end of the chapter, Smith says she once prayed for "paths so dark that an utter abandonment of trust might be my blessed and glorious privilege." Most people pray for easier paths, not harder ones. What would it mean to view difficulty not as an obstacle to faith but as the very occasion that makes deep trust possible — and do you find that vision attractive or alarming?
