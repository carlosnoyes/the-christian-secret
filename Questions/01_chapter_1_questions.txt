Chapter 1: God's Side and Man's Side — Discussion Questions
============================================================

1. Smith insists that the clay's only job is to "lie passive" in the potter's hands — submitting to turnings, overturnings, tearing apart, and pressing together. In your actual life, what does it look like to remain yielded when God's process feels more like destruction than formation?

2. The chapter contrasts two women who received similar outpourings of the Spirit: one was set to sweeping and baking and accepted it; the other was set to nursing peevish relatives and rebelled, losing her blessing entirely. What makes the difference between a person who can receive mundane or frustrating assignments as God's training and one who resists — and is that capacity itself something we cultivate or something given to us?

3. Smith uses the image of a June apple that is "perfect for June" even though it is far from the October apple. How does this idea sit with you when applied to real people — can we genuinely regard someone as spiritually "perfect at their stage" while they still carry habits or attitudes that harm others?

4. The chapter argues that most confusion about the Christian life comes from seeing only one side — either God's sovereignty or human responsibility — and mistaking it for the whole. Which side have you personally tended to over-emphasize, and what concrete consequences has that imbalance produced in how you relate to God or approach your own growth?

5. Smith says "all efforts after self-creating result in the marring of the vessel," yet most of us have been deeply shaped by disciplines we chose — education, counseling, habit-building. Where, if anywhere, do you draw the line between the kind of human effort that cooperates with God's work and the self-effort Smith warns actually hinders it?
