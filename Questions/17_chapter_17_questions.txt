Chapter 17: Oneness with Christ — Discussion Questions
=======================================================

1. Smith insists that oneness with Christ is measured not by ecstatic emotions but by Christlike character — that "unless we are Christ-like in our thoughts and our ways, we are not one with Him, no matter how we feel." How does this standard challenge the common evangelical emphasis on the quality of one's "personal relationship with Jesus" as primarily an emotional or devotional experience?

2. The chapter describes Christians with "hardly one Christ-like attribute in their whole characters" who yet had such powerful emotional experiences that they believed themselves deeply united with Christ. Smith calls this "a sadder sight." Have you seen this disconnect in others or in yourself — and what makes it so easy to substitute strong feelings for genuine transformation?

3. Smith pictures Christ living in a house, "shut up in a far-off closet, unknown and unnoticed," longing to be invited into every room. She says this describes even genuine believers who have never realized His indwelling presence. What "rooms" of your life — daily routines, relationships, thought patterns, decisions — have you not yet opened to His presence, and what would it mean to invite Him in?

4. Jesus proved His oneness with the Father not by what He felt but by what He did: "If I do not the works of my Father, believe me not." Smith says the test of oneness is always the doing of the same works. If someone evaluated your oneness with Christ purely by observing your actions over the past month — without access to your feelings, prayers, or beliefs — what would they conclude?

5. The chapter outlines three simple steps to union: be convinced from Scripture of God's indwelling, surrender your whole being to Him, and believe He has taken possession. Smith says "it is such a simple transaction, and yet so real." Why do you think something so simple remains so elusive for most Christians — what is it about us that resists the very thing we say we most desire?
