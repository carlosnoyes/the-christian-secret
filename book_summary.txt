THE CHRISTIAN'S SECRET OF A HAPPY LIFE
By Hannah Whitall Smith

Complete Book Summary
=====================


OVERVIEW
--------

"The Christian's Secret of a Happy Life" is a practical guide to what Hannah
Whitall Smith calls the "Higher Christian Life" -- a life of faith, rest, and
joy in God. Written not as a theological treatise but as a personal testimony,
Smith addresses the "struggling, weary ones in the Church of Christ" who long
for victory over sin, freedom from anxiety, and a deeper walk with God. Her
central message is that the secret to a happy Christian life lies in complete
surrender to God and absolute trust in His faithfulness. Man's part is simply
to trust; God's part is to work. When these two sides are held together,
the believer enters into a life of peace, power, and practical holiness.

The book unfolds in a clear progression: it first establishes the scriptural
foundation for this life, then defines what it is, explains how to enter it,
addresses the practical difficulties believers encounter along the way, and
finally describes the heights of joy and union with Christ that await those
who persevere in faith.


PART ONE: THE FOUNDATION (Chapters 1-4)
----------------------------------------

Smith begins by identifying the root cause of confusion about the life of
faith: people tend to see only one side of the truth. In Chapter 1, she
establishes the book's governing principle -- that sanctification has two
distinct but complementary sides. Man's part is to trust, and God's part is
to work. She illustrates this with the potter and the clay: the clay's only
role is to lie passive in the potter's hands, while the potter does all the
shaping, kneading, and firing. Sanctification is both a sudden step of faith
(man's side) and a gradual process of transformation (God's side). Just as
a June apple is a perfect apple for June even though it will ripen further by
October, a new believer who has taken the step of faith is perfect at that
stage even while God continues His refining work.

Chapter 2 marshals extensive scriptural evidence that this life of victory
over sin is not a novel invention but a thoroughly biblical reality. Smith
argues that Christ's death was meant to deliver believers not only from the
guilt of sin but from its power and dominion. She cites passages from Matthew,
Luke, Acts, Romans, Ephesians, Titus, and 1 Peter, along with voices from
church history including Dr. Thomas Chalmers and a seventeenth-century divine,
to show that God always intended complete deliverance for His people. A
redeemed captive left in fetters, she argues, would be a fraud -- and God is
no fraud.

In Chapter 3, Smith defines the life in its simplest terms: it is handing
over every burden to the Lord and leaving it there, living as a carefree child
in the Father's house. The greatest burden we carry is ourselves -- our
temperaments, weaknesses, feelings, and inward struggles -- and this must be
the first thing surrendered. She illustrates with the story of a man riding
in a wagon who kept his heavy load on his shoulders because he felt it was
too much to ask the driver to carry both him and his burden. The command
"Be careful for nothing" (Philippians 4:6) covers everything, with no
exceptions.

Chapter 4 lays out the two practical steps for entering this life:
consecration and faith. Consecration means placing spirit, soul, and body
under God's absolute control, holding nothing back. Faith means believing that
God accepts what is given and begins His work now -- not in some vague future,
but in the present tense. Smith tells the story of little Charley and his
mother: a Christian woman was afraid to say "Thy will be done" until her
friend asked how she would feel if her son said, "Mother, I will let you
have your own way with me." She realized she would shower him with love, not
hardship -- and that God is even more tender. Another illustration captures
the leap of faith: a man descending a well on a rope that seemed too short
let go in terror, only to fall three inches to the bottom.


PART TWO: PRACTICAL DIFFICULTIES (Chapters 5-8)
------------------------------------------------

Having explained how to enter the life of faith, Smith devotes the next
section to the difficulties that most commonly hinder believers.

Chapter 5 addresses difficulties concerning consecration. The chief problem
is that believers wait to feel consecrated before believing it has happened,
when God's order is always faith first, feeling second. Smith compares
repeated re-consecration to giving an estate to a friend and then returning
day after day to re-give it -- it creates endless confusion and dishonors
both parties. Under Old Testament law, every devoted thing became "most holy
unto the Lord" by the act of giving (Leviticus 27:28), regardless of the
giver's feelings. The holiness came from the Receiver, not the giver's
sincerity.

Chapter 6 tackles difficulties concerning faith. Smith argues that faith is
not a mysterious religious feeling but simply believing God -- just as sight
is simply seeing. She points out that people already exercise enormous trust
in daily life, trusting cooks, train conductors, and nurses without a second
thought, yet claim they cannot trust God. She illustrates with the story of
the emancipation of the slaves: the government proclaimed the slaves free,
and their freedom was a fact from the moment of the Proclamation, but they
only experienced it when they believed it. One old woman remained in slavery
for years after the war because her master denied the news and she was afraid
to believe. Smith establishes the divine order: first the Fact, then the
Faith, then the Feeling.

Chapter 7 addresses difficulties concerning the will. Drawing on Fenelon's
principle that "pure religion resides in the will alone," Smith teaches that
the life of faith is lived in the will, not in the emotions. The will is the
"king" of one's nature, and when the will is surrendered to God, the rest
must eventually follow. She tells of a young man enslaved by habitual doubt
who was told to put his will on the believing side and say "I will believe,
I do believe," regardless of his emotions. After a few days of steadfastly
holding to his will's decision, the Holy Spirit brought every thought into
captivity. She also shares the letter of William Hill, a Presbyterian minister
who in 1842 made a covenant of consecration with "a heart full of hardness
and darkness" and found steady victory by walking by faith, not feelings.

Chapter 8 confronts the difficulty of seeing God in everything, especially
in trials that come through human instrumentality. Smith argues that for the
children of God there are no "second causes" -- everything that reaches them
has first passed through God's hands. She points to Joseph, whose brothers
meant evil but who could say, "God sent me" (Genesis 45:5-8; 50:20). She
recounts the story of a lady who received a vision of God's advancing
presence enveloping everything -- fighting armies, wicked men, storms, even
petty annoyances. Her difficulty vanished forever. And she tells of Nancy,
a joyous woman who, when a gloomy friend listed all the things that might go
wrong, cried: "Stop! I never supposes. De Lord is my shepherd, and I knows I
shall not want."


PART THREE: THE LIFE IN PRACTICE (Chapters 9-15)
-------------------------------------------------

The middle section of the book addresses how the life of faith works out in
the believer's daily experience.

Chapter 9 teaches that true spiritual growth happens not by striving but by
being planted in the grace of God, like a lily resting in the sunshine. Smith
tells of a lady who had been "growing" for twenty-five years yet was less
devoted than at the start -- illustrating the futility of self-effort. The
believer's role is to abide in the Vine and remove barriers; the Divine
Husbandman handles the cultivating, pruning, and all circumstances.

Chapter 10 addresses Christian service, which becomes a burden and a treadmill
when driven by duty alone. When the believer surrenders the will to God, He
works within to want to do His will, transforming service from bondage into
freedom. Smith illustrates with the story of a girl in an institution for
people with disabilities: she had no strength of her own but made perfect
movements in a dumb-bell exercise because the master stood behind her and
did it all. Her utter weakness was her greatest strength.

Chapter 11 provides practical guidance for discerning God's leading. The
fully surrendered believer can expect divine guidance but must test all
impressions against four harmonizing voices: Scripture, the inward impressions
of the Holy Spirit, sanctified judgment or common sense, and providential
circumstances. Where all four agree, it is safe to act; where they do not,
one must wait.

Chapter 12 corrects the common mistake of expecting freedom from temptation
in the life of faith. In reality, temptations generally increase, just as
Israel faced far more enemies inside the Promised Land than in the
wilderness. But temptation is not sin -- it becomes sin only when we
entertain it and give half-consent of the will. The enemy's chief strategy
is to make believers think the temptation itself is their sin, plunging them
into discouragement. Smith declares that "all discouragement is from the
devil" and that cheerfulness is one of the most helpful things in the
spiritual life. She tells of an invalid lady who clipped open a butterfly's
cocoon to help the struggling creature, only to find the wings came out
lifeless -- the struggle was needed to send life-fluid into the veins.

Chapter 13 addresses failures. When a believer sins, two dangerous responses
must be avoided: utter discouragement or covering the sin up. The proper
response is to confess immediately, believe in God's instant forgiveness
(1 John 1:9), and trust more fully than before. Smith points to Israel's
defeat at Ai, which was caused not by the enemy's strength but by Achan's
hidden sin -- once the accursed thing was removed, victory followed. She
tells of a discouraged man who spiraled into three years of despair after one
unkind act, until a Christian lady showed him 1 John 1:9 and he claimed
forgiveness by faith in minutes. A moth-infested cask in a cellar illustrates
how hidden, unexamined sins cause ongoing spiritual defeat.

Chapter 14 treats doubting as a sinful habit requiring a total-abstinence
pledge, not a trial to endure. Doubting is not humility -- it is indulging
in hard thoughts about God. Smith tells of two little girls left at her
house: one played happily while her mother was away, the other sat in a
corner convinced her mother had forgotten her. The mother's grief-stricken
response upon return powerfully illustrated how God feels about our doubts.
She also recounts her young daughter Ray's battles with Satan's doubts:
"Satan, I will believe it. So there!" and "Satan, shut thy mouth!"

Chapter 15 insists that the life of faith must produce visible, practical
results. The world reads lives rather than Bibles, and the inward life must
manifest outwardly in Christlike character. Smith lists specific marks:
meekness, quietness of spirit, sweetness under provocation, calmness amid
turmoil, freedom from worry and fear. A thoughtful Hindu visitor to England
and America concluded: "What Christians need is a little more of Christ's
Christianity, and a little less of man's."


PART FOUR: THE HEIGHTS OF JOY AND UNION (Chapters 16-22)
---------------------------------------------------------

The final section of the book describes the deeper joys and richer
experiences that await those who persevere in the life of faith.

Chapter 16 presents total obedience as the highest joy, not a burden.
"Perfect obedience would be perfect happiness, if only we had perfect
confidence in the power we were obeying." Smith distinguishes between
friendship and love: friendship allows separate interests and moderate
devotion, but love demands total union where all belongs to both. God's love
for the believer is like a bridegroom's love for his bride, and the soul that
yields completely discovers unspeakable delight. Even a poor woman at her
wash-tub, living in Christ and following His leading, would find every hour
glorious.

Chapter 17 teaches that true oneness with Christ is measured not by emotional
experiences but by Christlike character. If there is no likeness of character,
unity of aim, and similarity of thought and action, there can be no real
oneness. The test of union is doing the same works as Christ, just as Jesus
proved His oneness with the Father by doing the Father's works. Three
practical steps are given: be convinced from Scripture of God's indwelling,
surrender your whole being to Him, and believe He has taken possession.

Chapter 18 uses Habakkuk 3:17-18 -- "Although the fig-tree shall not blossom
... yet I will rejoice in the Lord" -- to teach that the soul must learn to
rejoice in God alone, not in feelings, experiences, or answered prayers. Every
advancing soul must eventually trust in the "bare God," stripped of all
sensible consolations. The spiritual pathway has three regions: a region of
beginnings full of sensible joys, a vast wilderness of trial and dryness, and
mountain heights of uninterrupted union with God.

Chapter 19 explores the interior kingdom of God, which is set up within the
soul, not displayed outwardly. True kingship is exercised through meekness,
service, childlikeness, and self-sacrifice -- the opposite of the world's
idea of ruling. Smith tells of the monk of St. Cyr who endured cruel
persecution with unfailing kindness until he won over his enemies, and of
Joseph reigning as a king in slavery and prison before ascending to Pharaoh's
throne. Entry into this kingdom requires becoming as a little child.

Chapter 20 teaches that every trial is actually one of "God's chariots,"
sent to carry the soul to higher places. Psalm 68:17 declares that the
chariots of God are "twenty thousand, even thousands of angels." What appears
to be suffering and defeat is a chariot of triumph in disguise. Smith tells
of a lady with a very slow servant who had prayed for patience: she realized
the slow servant was God's chariot to carry her into that very patience. She
also points to Elisha's servant who saw only the Syrian army until God opened
his eyes to the mountain full of horses and chariots of fire (2 Kings 6).

Chapter 21 addresses the common divorce between "spiritual" life and
"temporal" life, arguing it is one of the greatest hindrances to union with
God. Since Christ declared "Without me ye can do nothing" (John 15:5), every
act of daily life -- housekeeping, business, social duties -- must be done in
dependence on Him. Smith tells of a young Christian mother overwhelmed by
household duties who felt separated from God all day; when told to see God in
her homely duties, her life became "a transformed and glorified thing." The
kitchen, workshop, nursery, and parlor are as sacred as the prayer meeting.

The book closes with Chapter 22, an exposition of Psalm 139 centered on the
truth of "Emmanuel, God with us." God's continual presence is not a beautiful
sentiment but an incontrovertible fact. Smith recalls her childhood: whenever
her father or mother arrived saying "Daughter, I am here," every burden
dropped and every anxiety was stilled. Their simple presence was all the
assurance she needed. How much more should the presence of our Heavenly
Father quiet every fear. She tells of Old Betty, a poor ale-seller on an
English moor, who upon hearing of God's presence immediately tipped her pot
of ale into the road because "The Lord will not like to have that ale in the
house where He lives." The practical application is simple: convince yourself
from Scripture that God's presence is a fact, practice His presence always,
acknowledge Him in all your ways, and do everything for His glory.


THE SECRET IN A SENTENCE
-------------------------

The Christian's secret of a happy life is this: abandon yourself entirely to
God, believe that He has accepted you and is at work in you, and then rest in
His love and faithfulness -- trusting Him with everything, obeying Him in
everything, and seeing Him in everything -- and the result will be a life of
peace, power, and joy that no outward circumstance can shake.


KEY THEMES THROUGHOUT THE BOOK
-------------------------------

1. Trust and Surrender: Man's sole responsibility is to trust God completely
   and surrender the will to Him. God's responsibility is to do the work of
   transformation.

2. Faith over Feeling: God's order is always Fact, Faith, Feeling -- never
   the reverse. Believers must act on what God has said, not on what they
   feel.

3. God in Everything: For the children of God, there are no second causes.
   Every circumstance, trial, and difficulty has passed through God's hands
   and can be received as coming from Him.

4. The Will as the True Self: The life of faith resides in the will, not the
   emotions. When the will is set on God's side, the transaction is real
   regardless of how the feelings rebel.

5. Childlike Dependence: The model for the Christian life is a little child
   resting in its father's care -- providing nothing for itself, taking no
   thought for the morrow, trusting completely.

6. Practical Holiness: The life of faith must produce visible results in
   daily living -- Christlike character, selfless service, and freedom from
   anxiety, doubt, and worldliness.

7. God's Continual Presence: God is not far off but always with us and within
   us. Practicing awareness of His presence is the foundation of peace and
   the solution to every difficulty.


NOTABLE ILLUSTRATIONS
---------------------

- The Potter and the Clay (Ch. 1): The clay's only role is to lie passive;
  the potter does all the work.
- The Man in the Wagon (Ch. 3): He kept his burden on his shoulders instead
  of setting it down, not wanting to trouble the driver.
- Little Charley (Ch. 4): A mother realizes she would rejoice, not punish,
  if her child gave her his will -- and God is more tender still.
- The Well Rope (Ch. 4): A terrified man lets go of a rope in a dark well
  and falls only three inches.
- The Emancipation of the Slaves (Ch. 6): Freedom was a fact before it was
  felt -- the divine order of Fact, Faith, Feeling.
- Nancy (Ch. 8): "I never supposes. De Lord is my shepherd."
- The Butterfly Cocoon (Ch. 12): Cutting the struggle short produced lifeless
  wings -- temptation strengthens the soul.
- The Moth-Infested Cask (Ch. 13): Hidden sin, like an unexamined cask in
  the cellar, is the source of ongoing defeat.
- The Two Little Girls (Ch. 14): One trusted; the other doubted -- and the
  mother's heart broke for the doubter.
- The Slow Servant (Ch. 20): God's chariot of patience, disguised as an
  annoying trial.
- Old Betty (Ch. 22): She poured out the ale because the Lord would not like
  it in His house.
